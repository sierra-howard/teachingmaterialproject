U.S. History – Reconstruction in South Carolina

From Bondage to Ballot Box

Unit Purpose

Explore how African Americans in South Carolina transformed politics, education, and community life during Reconstruction – and how violent resistance and federal retreat reshaped those gains.

  • Center African American agency using diverse primary sources.
  • Practice historical thinking (sourcing, context, corroboration, close reading, significance).
  • Connect Reconstruction's legacy to today's civil rights issues.

Standards & Outcomes

USHC.2.CX • USHC.2.CC • USHC.2.E

  • Analyze 8+ primary sources from South Carolina during Reconstruction.
  • Build evidence-based arguments about federal protection of civil rights.
  • Explain the rise & fall of African American political power, 1865–1877.
Primary sources include the 1868 S.C. Constitution debates, Freedmen's Bureau records, Klan hearings, Charlotte Forten Grimké's journals, and speeches by Robert Smalls.

Day 1
Direct Instruction

Reconstruction Begins: Bureau, Labor, Family

Context: When the Civil War ended in April 1865, South Carolina faced unprecedented transformation. Nearly 400,000 formerly enslaved people—about 60% of the state's population—suddenly gained freedom.

The Freedmen's Bureau

  • Established March 1865, led by General Oliver O. Howard
  • Only ~100 agents covering all of South Carolina
  • Mission: distribute food, negotiate labor contracts, establish schools, legitimize marriages

Defining Freedom - Three Immediate Priorities:

  • Family reunification: People walked hundreds of miles searching for sold-away family members. Bureau ads: "Sam Johnson seeks his wife Mary and children, last seen in Charleston 1862."
  • Fair wages: After generations of unpaid labor, freedpeople demanded cash wages and written contracts
  • Mobility: The ability to move freely became powerful—many left plantations simply to prove they could

Key point: The Bureau faced an impossible task with limited resources, trying to protect freedpeople's rights while maintaining agricultural production.

Day 1
Direct Instruction

Black Codes & Labor Systems in S.C. (1865–1866)

South Carolina's Black Codes (December 1865)

  • Vagrancy laws: Any Black person without "lawful employment" could be arrested, fined, and hired out to pay the fine
  • License requirements: Black artisans needed expensive licenses to practice trades
  • Apprenticeship laws: Courts could bind Black children to white "masters" without parental consent
  • Firearms prohibition: Black citizens couldn't own military-grade weapons

"The white people tell us we are free, but we see no difference yet." - Freedman's testimony, 1866

Emergence of Sharecropping

  • Compromise between planters (land but no cash) and freedpeople (wanted autonomy but lacked capital)
  • System: Planters provide land/seed/tools; Workers provide labor; Crop split 50-50 or 60-40
  • Workers bought supplies on credit from plantation stores
  • The trap: Debt accumulation meant a bad crop year could leave families owing more than earned, legally bound to work another year
Day 1
Primary Source Insight

Labor Contract – Orangeburg District (1866)

  • Terms: Monthly wages and rations; deductions for missed days; overseer authority spelled out.
  • Control: Clauses limiting travel without a pass; penalties for "idleness."
  • Signatures: Many workers signed with an X, witnessed by a Bureau agent.
  • Takeaway: Contracts provided pay and recognition but preserved planter power.
Day 1
Primary Source Insight

Marriage Certificates & Family Reunification

  • Legal family: Bureau-registered marriages turned long-standing unions into recognized families.
  • Names: Couples formalized surnames; certificates often list children.
  • Protection: Recognition helped secure wages, inheritance, and custody.
  • Takeaway: Freedom meant belonging to a family under law.

  • Black Codes – State/local laws restricting Black freedom and labor options in 1865–66.
  • Sharecropping – Labor system exchanging work for a portion of the crop rather than wages.

Day 2
Direct Instruction

Convention & Leadership

Revolutionary moment: The Reconstruction Act of 1867 transformed S.C. politics. Black men could vote while many former Confederates could not. Result: 76 of 124 convention delegates were African American.

Key Leaders

  • Robert Smalls: Born enslaved in Beaufort. Commandeered Confederate ship Planter in 1862, delivered to Union. By 1868, Republican delegate advocating for education and civil rights. Served 5 terms in Congress.
  • Joseph Rainey: Georgetown barber who fled to Bermuda during war. Returned to help write constitution. First African American U.S. Representative from South Carolina.
  • Francis Cardozo: Free-born Charleston, educated at University of Glasgow. As Secretary of State and State Treasurer, fought corruption and established financial accountability.

Quote from William Beverly Nash (delegate): "We are ready because we have been preparing all our lives. Who knows better than the slave what freedom means?"

Note: Tensions existed between Black delegates, white Republicans ("scalawags"), and Northern Republicans ("carpetbaggers") over pace of reform.

Day 2
Direct Instruction

What's New in the 1868 S.C. Constitution?

Public Education (Article X)

  • First time S.C. took responsibility for educating all children
  • Mandated "uniform system of free public schools"
  • By 1870: 900 schools serving 50,000 Black children (up from zero in 1865)

Universal Male Suffrage

  • Eliminated all property requirements for voting/officeholding
  • Any male citizen 21+ could vote, regardless of race or wealth
  • Expanded electorate from ~45,000 to over 140,000 voters

Civil Rights Protections (Article I)

"Distinction on account of race or color, in any case whatever, shall be prohibited, and all classes of citizens shall enjoy equally all common, public, legal and political privileges."

Representation Changes

  • Districts redrawn by population, not wealth
  • Power shifted from lowcountry planters to upcountry and Black-majority areas
  • By 1870: African Americans held 85 of 157 legislative seats
Day 2
Primary Source Insight

Debate: Education Clause

  • Problem: Wartime/destruction left few schools; who should pay?
  • Proposal: Tax-funded, state-run system to reach rural communities.
  • Opposition: Cost and control; fears of educating formerly enslaved people.
  • Outcome: Education becomes a state responsibility for the first time.
Day 2
Primary Source Insight

Debate: Suffrage & Civil Rights

  • Argument for suffrage: Citizenship demands the ballot; protection from local abuse.
  • Counter-claims: Claims about "readiness" and property; racialized fears.
  • Civil rights: Contracts, testimony in court, and equal protection contested then affirmed.
Day 2

Leaders & Firsts

  • Robert Smalls: Civil War hero; later state & U.S. representative; advocate for equal rights.
  • Joseph Rainey: First Black member of the U.S. House from S.C.; supported civil rights legislation.
  • Francis Cardozo: State treasurer and educator; helped build the school system.

Day 3
Direct Instruction

Schools & Sharecropping

Charlotte Forten (1862): "It is wonderful how a people who have been so long crushed to the earth...can have so great a desire for knowledge."

Educational Institutions by 1870

  • Penn School (St. Helena Island): Founded 1862, model for freedpeople's education
  • Avery Institute (Charleston): Classical education including Latin and Greek
  • Claflin University (Orangeburg): One of first colleges for African Americans in state

Community-Driven Education

  • Adults attended night schools after working all day
  • Families pooled $1-2/month for teachers and church rentals
  • Bureau report: "Freedpeople are building schoolhouses with their own hands, buying books with money earned by extra labor"

Sharecropping Economics (Example)

  • Average family: 20-30 acres cotton
  • Good year: 10 bales @ $50 = $500 gross
  • Landowner's 50% = $250
  • Family's share = $250
  • Minus: seed/tools ($40) + supplies/credit ($150) + interest (25%) = $230 expenses
  • Net income: $20 for entire year
Day 3
Deep Dive

Inside a Reconstruction Classroom

  • Teachers: Northern missionaries and local Black teachers partnered with communities.
  • Conditions: Crowded rooms, scarce books; classes in churches and homes.
  • Community: Parents pooled funds; night schools for adults; high attendance despite distance.
Day 3
Worked Example

Sharecropping Ledger Math

Example: 1,000 lbs cotton at $0.12/lb = $120 gross

  • Landowner takes 1/2 share → $60 to landowner, $60 to family.
  • Deduct seed & tools ($18) + store credit with interest ($12) from family share.
  • Net to family: $60 − $30 = $30 for the season.
  • Discussion: How do debt and prices trap families in cycles?
Day 3
Geography

Where Were the Schools?

  • Early centers: Beaufort/Sea Islands (Penn School), Charleston (Avery Institute).
  • Rural expansion: Church-based classes in the Midlands and Upcountry.
  • Barriers: Distance, funding, and threats against teachers and students.
Day 3
Key Terms

Know the Language

  • Tuition schools – Community-funded classes before full state systems matured.
  • Peonage/debt cycle – Owing the store/landowner keeps families tied to land.
  • Aid societies – Northern groups that financed teachers and materials.

Day 4
Direct Instruction

Violence, Investigation, Intervention

Context: By 1868, the Ku Klux Klan (founded Tennessee 1866) established chapters throughout S.C.'s upcountry.

Pattern of Terror

  • Timing: Attacks increased before elections
  • Targets: Black officeholders, teachers, ministers, voting advocates
  • Methods: Night raids, whippings, shootings, lynchings
  • Scale: York County alone - 11 murders, 600 whippings in 6 months

Elias Hill's Testimony (1871)

Hill, disabled Black minister in York County, dragged from cabin, beaten, threatened with death for preaching about voting rights. His crime: Teaching that Constitution guaranteed equal rights.

Congressional Response

  • Joint Select Committee collected 12 volumes of testimony
  • Harriet Simril testified Klansmen killed her husband for voting Republican: "This is what happens to radicals"
  • Investigation revealed organized political terrorism led by prominent citizens—doctors, lawyers, planters
Day 4
Primary Source Insight

Elias Hill's Testimony

  • Targets: Black leaders, teachers, officeholders, and voters.
  • Methods: Night raids, beatings, threats to stop voting or teaching.
  • Purpose: Break political participation and restore white supremacist control.
  • Evidence skill: Identify who acted, what they demanded, and how victims responded.
Day 4
Direct Instruction

Enforcement Acts & Federal Power

Three Enforcement Acts (1870-1871)

  • Made it federal crime to interfere with voting rights
  • Criminalized conspiracies to deprive constitutional rights
  • Allowed going in disguise to intimidate voters to be prosecuted

Grant's Unprecedented Action

  • October 1871: Suspended habeas corpus in 9 S.C. counties
  • Only peacetime suspension in American history
  • Federal troops arrested 500+ Klansmen
  • U.S. Attorney David Corbin prosecuted in federal court
  • Violence decreased dramatically in 1872

Obstacles to Enforcement

  • Limited resources: Only 1,100 federal troops for all S.C.
  • Legal challenges: Defense argued federal overreach
  • Northern fatigue: By 1874, public opinion shifted
  • Local resistance: White juries rarely convicted

Critical ruling: U.S. v. Cruikshank (1876) - Federal government could only prosecute state actors, not individuals. This ended federal protection until 1960s.

Day 5
Direct Instruction

Compromise, Retrenchment, Disenfranchisement

The Compromise of 1877

Disputed 1876 election: Democrat Tilden won popular vote, 20 electoral votes contested (including S.C.)

  • Deal: Republican Hayes becomes president
  • Federal troops withdraw from Southern states
  • Democrats ("Redeemers") regain control of S.C.

Wade Hampton's False Promise

Confederate general turned governor promised to respect Black rights, but implemented "Mississippi Plan":

  • Economic pressure: Fire Black workers who vote Republican
  • Rifle clubs: Paramilitary intimidation
  • Fraud: Ballot stuffing, discarded Republican votes

Rapid Decline

  • 1870: 85 African Americans in state legislature
  • 1878: Only 3 remain
  • 1877: Federal troops leave
  • 1878: Democrats control all state offices
  • 1882: Eight-box law makes voting nearly impossible
  • 1890s: Lynchings average one per month
Day 5
Direct Instruction

What Changed After 1877?

Political Reversals

  • Black officeholding: 300+ officials (1870) → fewer than 10 (1890)
  • One-party Democratic rule until 1960s
  • Local offices purged of Black members

Educational Decline

  • Black school funding cut 75%
  • Teacher salaries: White $35/month, Black $15/month
  • Black illiteracy rose from 30% (1880) to 52% (1900)

Economic & Social Control

  • Sharecropping expanded, debt cycles deepened
  • Convict lease system re-enslaved thousands
  • Black land ownership peaked 14% (1890), then declined
  • Lynchings became public spectacles
  • "Whitecapping" drove successful Black farmers away

Resistance Continues

  • Churches: centers of community organization
  • Mutual aid societies: insurance and loans
  • Secret political meetings despite risks
Day 5
Direct Instruction

1895 Constitution: How Voting Was Curtailed

Governor "Pitchfork" Ben Tillman's goal: Legally disenfranchise Black voters without mentioning race (to avoid violating 15th Amendment)

The Obstacle Course

  1. Residence: 2 years state, 1 year county, 4 months precinct
  2. Poll tax: $1 annually ($35 today), paid 6 months before election
  3. Literacy test OR understanding clause: Read/write Constitution OR explain when read aloud
  4. Property alternative: Own $300 property (instead of literacy)

The Trap: "Understanding clause" gave registrars complete discretion. White illiterates "understood" simple passages; educated Blacks failed complex sections.

Robert Smalls' Warning

Only Black delegate at 1895 convention: "You are disenfranchising your own people as well as mine. Poor whites will suffer alongside freedmen."

Devastating Results

  • 1896: 130,000 Black voters
  • 1898: 15,000 Black voters
  • 1940: 3,000 Black voters (<1% of eligible)

Smalls' final words: "This convention has been called to disenfranchise the Negro and commit a great wrong. Time will right this injustice."
